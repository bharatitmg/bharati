How NASA Has Prepared to Scoop Up an Asteroid Sample Landing in the Desert

Introduction:
NASA is on the verge of a historic moment as it prepares to retrieve a valuable asteroid sample that has been on a remarkable journey through space. The OSIRIS-REx mission, which stands for Origins, Spectral Interpretation, Resource Identification, Security, Regolith Explorer, is set to drop off a sample of rocks and soil from the near-Earth asteroid Bennu in the Utah desert on September 24. This mission represents NASA's first attempt at collecting and returning an asteroid sample from space, and it holds immense scientific promise. In this blog, we will explore how NASA has meticulously prepared for this momentous event.

The OSIRIS-REx Mission:
The OSIRIS-REx mission was launched in 2016 and began its journey to the asteroid Bennu in 2018. Its primary goal was to collect a pristine sample from the asteroid, shedding light on the formation of our solar system approximately 4.5 billion years ago. This mission marks a significant milestone in space exploration and has required years of planning and preparation.

The Risk of Contamination:
One of the major challenges in this mission is to prevent contamination of the asteroid sample. If the capsule carrying the sample crash-lands and opens unexpectedly, the precious material could become compromised. To mitigate this risk, NASA has conducted extensive rehearsals and prepared for various scenarios to ensure the safe retrieval of the sample.

Precision and Timing:
On September 24, NASA will live-stream the capsule's descent to Earth. The decision to release the capsule will depend on several factors, including the spacecraft's trajectory, the safety of the landing zone, reentry temperature, and accuracy. The capsule will be released when OSIRIS-REx is 63,000 miles from Earth, aiming for a landing zone spanning 250 square miles.

Protecting the Sample:
As the capsule enters Earth's atmosphere, it will be engulfed in a superhot ball of fire, but its heat shield will protect the valuable sample inside. Parachutes will deploy to slow down the capsule's descent to a gentle 11 miles per hour. Recovery teams will be on standby to retrieve the capsule once it's safe.

Secure Transportation:
After landing, a helicopter will transport the sample in a cargo net to a temporary cleanroom in Utah. From there, it will be transported via a C-17 aircraft to NASA's Johnson Space Center in Houston. Details about the sample will be shared with the public through a NASA broadcast on October 11.

Analyzing the Sample:
NASA and its international partners, including the Canadian Space Agency and Japanese Aerospace Exploration Agency, will analyze the asteroid sample. This material, some of which is smaller than a grain of sand, will provide insights into the formation and history of our solar system, including the role of asteroids in delivering essential elements like water to Earth.

Conclusion:
The OSIRIS-REx mission represents a remarkable achievement in space exploration, and NASA's meticulous preparation underscores the significance of this endeavor. The asteroid sample's safe return to Earth holds the potential to unlock secrets about our solar system's origins and the role of asteroids in shaping our planet's history. As we eagerly await this historic event, we acknowledge the dedication and hard work of the NASA and Lockheed Martin Space teams that have made it possible.
